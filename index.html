Dr. Gaurav Ahuja

Assistant Professor (Computational Biology)
PhD (2015), University of Cologne, Germany

Gaurav Ahuja graduated summa cum laude from the University of Cologne, Germany with PhD in Natural Sciences. He is a molecular biologist by training and possesses a vast experience in functional genomics, especially in dissecting complex biological mechanisms using genome-editing and next generation sequencing-based techniques. After the completion of his PhD degree, he worked as a Post Doctoral Fellow at several renowned institutes, including Center for Molecular Medicine Cologne, Max Planck Institute for Ageing and Lee Kong Chian School of Medicine, Singapore. During this period, he worked on multiple model systems, including human pluripotent stem cells, zebrafish, mice and killifish.

Our Interest

Biological systems are endowed with the ability to selectively and precisely receive inputs from a multitude of diverse intrinsic or extrinsic clues, which in turn triggers a transient modulation of distinct gene regulatory networks by the combinatorial effect of genetic and epigenetic signatures, ultimately results in complex physiological or behavioral outputs. The underlying diversity and complexity of the aforementioned processes triggered my interest in chemosensation, where a complex behavioral output has been linked to the identity of the triggering molecule.

The Ahuja Lab at IIIT Delhi investigates chemosensation at various levels:

    Olfactogenomics

    Unlike other senses, such as vision, or taste, the molecular rules dictating the odor-evoked induction of complex behavioral responses are still elusive and therefore, provide a larger exploration space for the systematic decoding of this complex phenomenon. Interestingly, the expression of these olfactory receptors in the non-olfactory tissues (e.g., heart, brain, kidney, etc.) further highlights the overall complexity of the gene regulatory networks driving their expression in these tissues.


We aim to decode:

  • The underlying molecular mechanisms mediating the induction and regulation of ORs in non-olfactory tissues.
  • The functional relevance of these ectopic ORs in non-olfactory cells/tissue-types, in particular in Cancer.
  • The development of ectopic OR-based novel diagnostic or therapeutic strategies.
    • To achieve this, we effectively take advantage of both computational and molecular biology methods.


      Chemogenomics.

      Cellular functionalities and hence their underlying complexities are historically investigated around the central dogma of molecular biology. Recent advancements in high throughput metabolomics and their functional validations uncovered the hidden potential of metabolites, a class of chemical compounds that act as a substrate or product of metabolism and drive essential cellular functions. The metabolic diversity of any organism is highly complex because of parallel metabolite contributions from exogenous sources such as microorganisms, xenobiotics, dietary, etc. Our laboratory aims to dissect some of the fundamental aspects pertaining to the functional contributions of metabolites in driving essential cellular processes or behaviors. We use Saccharomyces cerevisiae as a model organism along with the computational methods to address the aforementioned objectives.


    Some of the key objectives include:


  • Developing assays or methods to link metabolites to cellular processes.
  • Decoding the chemical space of the human metabolome.


    • To achieve this, The Ahuja Lab effectively takes advantage of effectively takes advantage of both chemoinformatics, molecular biology, and genomics techniques.
